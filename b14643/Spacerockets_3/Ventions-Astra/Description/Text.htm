Astra Space, Inc. (Astra Space), formerly Ventions, LLC, is a San Francisco-based aerospace research and design firm with a ten-year history of developing aerospace technology in partnership with NASA. Ventions, LLC was founded in 2005 and reincorporated as Astra Space, Inc. in September 2016.
Astra Space is now in phase of development and flight-testing of a high-performance electric pump fed launch vehicle.
The project is focused on the development of a custom pump and electric motor capable of pressurizing propellants for an 8,000-10,000 lbf liquid bipropellant engine. Availability of such high-power pumps will not only allow for low pressure storage of propellants in light tanks for small-scale launch vehicle stages, but also provide electronic speed control with fast response times that may enable differential throttle control. The scope of the project includes design, fabrication, assembly and testing of a high-power electric pump capable of pressuring propellants to approximately 600 psi for an 8.000-10,000 lbf thrust engine.
 It is unlikely, however, that the current launcher match the advertised project, but rather represents is a XS-1 tactical missile with five RP-1/LOX engines (each of 4,000 lbf thrust) with total thrust of 89 kN. The future two-stage vehicle is to be able to transport 100 kg into a LEO.
The Astra LV was shown first for public in February 2018 at the Alameda Naval Air Station.

 

 

2022/2023
 -- Astra Space has inevitably halted production of its Rocket 3.3 and develops a new Rocket 4 with new purchased engines (Reaver, Hadley).
 

 

June 12, 2022 -- Unfortunately another failure. The second stage had a burn time that was 90 seconds too short and consequently the achieved final speed of 6,575 m/s was not sufficient for an orbit.

March 15, 2022 -- Second successful launch of Rocket 3.3.

February, 10, 2022 -- The third launch of a Rocket 3.3 ended in failure. The fairing did not separate and the second stage collided with the fairing.

November 20, 2021 -- First successful launch of Rocket 3.3. The rocket reached the required final speed and dropped its payload at an altitude of 496 km.

 

August 29, 2021 -- The first launch of the longer Rocket 3.3 was disastrous. The rocket initially drifted sideward and was ultimately no longer controllable despite stabilization.

 

December 15, 2020 -- Today's launch (Rocket 3.2) was at least a partial success. The second stage was in action for the first time, but did not reach the required final speed.

 

September 12, 2020 -- The next launch of the new two-stage Rocket .3.1 ended in a failure. The engines of the first stage switched off after 30 seconds, the rocket fell back and exploded on the ground.
 

March 23, 2020 -- The launch of the redesigned two-stage "Rocket 3.0" ended with the total destruction on the launch pad.

 

December 06, 2018 -- The second test flight of Astra's small launch vehicle “Rocket 2”ended in failure in late November from Kodiak. No details about the mission on Nov. 29 are known.
A launch license the FAA issued to Astra Space Inc. on Oct. 15, also available on the FAA website, authorized the company to perform a suborbital flight of its “Rocket 2” vehicle from Launch Pad 2 at Pacific Spaceport Complex-Alaska, the commercial launch site on Kodiak Island, Alaska, operated by Alaska Aerospace Corp.

As the name suggests, the launch was the second for Astra Space. The company performed an initial suborbital launch, also from Kodiak, July 21. The launch took place in foggy conditions and the outcome was also shrouded in secrecy: the FAA said that launch suffered an unspecified “mishap” but Alaska Aerospace Corp. said the customer for that launch, which it declined to disclose citing a nondisclosure agreement, was “very pleased with the outcome of the launch.”
The company conducted the first test launch at the spaceport in July  that was terminated 21 seconds into the rocket's flight. The rocket was terminated before it reached its suborbital destination, but the launch was still considered successful.
