Kistler  K-1
 
 

Kistler Aerospace Corporation

The K-1 is a new two-stage reusable launch vehicle being developed commercially by Kistler Aerospace Corporation. The K-1 can carry  payloads to 4.6 tons LEO and 1.25 tons SSO. Both stages are recovered using parachutes and airbags.
The first stage  is referred to as the Launch Assist Platform (LAP). The second stage is called the Orbital Vehicle (OV).
Spacecraft are carried in a reusable payload modul (PM) rather than a conventional payload fairing. Three types are available - the Sandard Payload Modul (SPM), Extended Payload Modul (EPM), and Active Dispenser Payload Module (ADPM).
Launch operations will be conducted from new launch facilities in Australia (Woomera) and Nevada (Nevada Test Site).
Both stage burn Kerosene/LOX propellants and are powered by modernized versions of Russian Kuznetsov NK-33 and NK-34 engines. The K-1 first stage engines derived from the NK-33 are designated the AJ-26-58 (single-start) or AJ-26-59 (restart). The second stage engine derived from the NK-43 is designated the AJ-26-60. An optional expendable third stage, fueled by MMH/N2O4, can be used for high-orbit missions.

The K-1 includes a new Orbital Maneuvering System (OMS) that burns Ethanol/LOX propellants. It is based on development work done at Aerojet  in the 1980s intended to create a nontoxic propulsion system to replace the Space Shuttle ACS engines use UDMH/N2O4 propellants. The K-1 OMS system consists of two spherical propellant tanks in the aft compartment of OV second stage.
The first stage  includes a smaller LOX retention tank inside the kerosene tank. This tank retains the LOX needed to restart the main engine for the return of the first stage to the launch site.
 

Orbital Sciences will oversee the final development, production and launch operations activities of Kistler's K-1 reusable launch vehicle system. Kistler’s K-1 vehicle was selected by the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) for the space agency's Commercial Orbital Transportation System (COTS) program to provide unmanned cargo transportation services to and from the International Space Station (ISS).
In implementing the COTS program, Kistler will contract with Orbital for the final development, integration, testing and first launches of the K-1 rocket to the ISS in 2008 and 2009. The company will build on the substantial development work already accomplished by the Kistler team over the past decade. Orbital's role in the program encompasses program management support to Kistler and lead responsibility for systems engineering and vehicle integration, launch operations, and safety and mission assurance.

In November 2007, PlanetSpace company, announced that it had teamed with aerospace giants Lockheed Martin and ATK to submit a proposal response for NASA's COTS (Commercial Orbital Transportation Services) program. The proposal was triggered by NASA's October 18, 2007 decision to cancel Rocketplane-Kistler's original COTS contract.
