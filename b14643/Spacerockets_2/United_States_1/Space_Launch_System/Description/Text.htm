Space Launch System (SLS)
 
 


         
Block-I (Artemis)

The Space Launch System ( SLS) is a Shuttle-Derived heavy launch vehicle being designed by NASA, following the cancellation of the Constellation Program, to replace the Space Shuttle after its retirement.
The NASA Authorization Act of 2010 envisions the transformation of the Ares I and Ares V vehicle designs into a single launch vehicle usable for both crew and cargo. It is to be upgraded over time with more powerful versions. The initial capability of the core elements, without an upper stage, should be for between 70 tons and 100 tons into LEO in preparation for transit for missions beyond low-Earth orbit. With the addition of integrated upper Earth departure stage the total lift capability of the Space Launch System should be 130 tons or more.
Preliminary designs indicate that the current Space Shuttle Main Engines and Space Shuttle Solid Rocket Boosters would be utilized, and much of the External Tank design would remain the same. This design would require dramatically less development time than the Ares V heavy lift launch vehicle. On May 24, 2011, NASA announced that development of the Orion from the Constellation program will continue under the name Multi-Purpose Crew Vehicle (MPCV).
Multi-Purpose Crew Vehicle (MPCV) is a planned spacecraft that is being designed by Lockheed Martin for NASA, the space agency of the United States, based on designs and tests already completed for the Orion spacecraft. The MPCV was annouced by NASA on 24 May 2011.
The MPCV spacecraft includes both crew and service modules, and a spacecraft adaptor. The MPCV is being developed for different crewed missions. The capsule is also planned as a backup vehicle for cargo and crewed missions to the International Space Station. It will be launched by the Space Launch System.

The SLS would be developed in two phases. Phase One would not include an upper stage. Phase Two would add an upper stage.
An "inline" core built using Shuttle External Tank (ET) tooling, powered by existing Space Shuttle Main Engines (RS-25D), boosted by a pair of five segment reusable solid rocket boosters (RSRBs).
In Phase Two the SLS could be augmented with a cryogenic upper stage. The stage would serve both to perform the final burn to reach LEO, and to provide beyond propulsion. The HEFT study left open the question of engine-type. The J-2X could it be, or a cluster of RL-10 derived "Next Generation Engines". The upper stage could boost 118 tonnes to LEO or more as 50 tonnes to escape velocity.

The SLS will use a liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen propulsion system, which will include the RS-25D/E from the Space Shuttle Program for the core stage and the Saturn V-derived J-2X rocket engine for the upper stage. However, initial development flights will also use solid rocket boosters. The SLS will have an initial lift capacity of 70 metric tons (154,324 lbs), which NASA points out is roughly the weight of 40 SUVs. An evolved SLS will then see lift capacity increased to 130 metric tons (286,600 lbs).
The initial SLS will weigh 2,500 metric tons and stand  97.5 m (320 ft) high. At liftoff it will generate 8.4 million pounds of thrust. The subsequent evolved SLS will weigh 2,950 metric tons and stand 122 m (400 ft) high. At liftoff it will generate 9.2 million pounds of thrust.

NASA says the SLS's architecture provides a launch vehicle that can be adapted to suit different missions through the use of different core stage, upper stage, and first-stage booster combinations. This flexibility is designed to allow it to more economically carry the Orion Multi-Purpose Crew Vehicle, as well as cargo, equipment and science experiments to as low as Earth's orbit and as far as Mars and beyond. While NASA is looking to save money by having private companies ferry astronauts to the International Space Station (ISS), the SLS will also serve as a backup for commercial and international partner transportation services to the ISS.

Project"Artemis"

2022, September 03 -- After two aborted launches due to technical problems, the date for another attempt is still unknown.

2022, August -- NASA’s Space Launch System will launch on its first test flight with an uncrewed Orion spacecraft on August 29. The mission, known as Artemis-1, will place the Orion spacecraft into orbit around the moon before the capsule returns to Earth for splashdown in the Pacific Ocean.

2022, July -- The launcher Artemis-1 has been back on the launch pad since some time to continue testing. The maiden flight is scheduled for August.

2022, March 28 - The planned full test was canceled due to technical problems at the rocket and the vehicle moved back for repairs.

2022, March 17 - NASA's unveiled its first Space Launch System rocket for the Artemis 1 mission. SLS 1/Artemis 1 rolled out of High Bay 3 of Kennedy Space Center's Vehicle Assembly Building (VAB), stacked atop new Mobile Launcher 1. The 1,570 tonne SLS 1/Artemis 1 stack was shouldered down the crawlerway by refurbished, 3,016.4 tonne Crawler Transporter 2 (CT 2). Rollout to Launch Complex 39B took 11.5 hours.

"Artemis-1" (previously Exploration Mission 1 (EM-1)) is the name for an unmanned flight of the US spacecraft Orion towards the moon. The mission is part of NASA's Artemis program and is scheduled to take place in 2022. The spaceship is scheduled to enter high lunar orbit and return to Earth, marking the first launch of Block-I of the Space Launch System (SLS)and its by Airbus-built Orion propulsion and supply unit ESM.
