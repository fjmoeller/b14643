The Atlas III  is intended to provide higher performance and reliability at a lower cost than the Atlas IIAS. This is to be accomplished primarily by simplifying and improving the propulsion systems. The characteristic stage-and-a-half main engine and the optional strap-on boosters are eliminated through the use of a new, high-performance RD-180 engine from Russia. The Atlas IIIA use a new single-engine Centaur.  For higher performance, the Atlas IIIB use a stretched Centaur that can use either one or two engines.

Derived from the RD-170 engine in the Zenit first stage, the RD-180 is a new twin-chambered engine with throttling capability. The  RD-180 is powered by a LOX-rich turbopump with a staged combustion cycle. This enables a very high chamber pressure, making the RD-180 one of the highest performance LOX/Kerosene engines in the world.
The new Atlas IIIA will use a single-engine Centaur (SEC) configuration. While the SEC provides improved performance to GTO through reduced weight, the lower thrust penalizes performance to LEO. Therefore, the Atlas IIIB will use a common Centaur design called the Centaur III that can be produced as either a SEC or DEC. The Centaur III has 1.7 m longer propellant tanks than the Centaurs used on Atlas IIA(S). The SEC version uses a single RL-10A-4-2 engine with electromechanical gimbal actuators. The DEC uses two engines with hydraulic gimbal actuators. The  RL-10A-4-2 has similar performance to the RL-10A-4-1 version, with operational and reliability.
The Atlas II and III series use three sizes of aluminium fairings. The large payload fairing (LPF) and extended payload fairing (EPF) are the most common. The EPF is 0.9 m (3 ft.) longer than the LPF, but is otherwise the same. The MPF fairing is shorter with 3.30 m diameter.
