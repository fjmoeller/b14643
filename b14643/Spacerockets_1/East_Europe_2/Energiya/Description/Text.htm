Energiya
 
 

RKK Energiya

 

Like the US Space Shuttle system, the Energiya was a two-stage system, but it was designed so that payloads (including a shuttle), mounted on the side or conventionally on the top, could be launched into orbit without the need for external engines. The Space Shuttle system, on the other hand, cannot take off without the main engines of an orbiter.
The first stage of the Energiya consists of four boosters, which are mounted in pairs around a core. The RD-170 (11D521) engine used in the boosters burns kerosene and liquid oxygen and produces a takeoff thrust of 7,256 kN (s.l.). The core is powered by 4 RD-0120 (11D122) engines with a total takeoff thrust of 5,804 kN (s.l.). They work with liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen. With a supply of 845 t of fuel, a burn time of 480 seconds is achieved.
The payload capacity of the Energiya is up to 105 t for a LEO. The first launch took place on May 15, 1987 with the piggyback payload "Polyus". The payload on Energia separates while the stack orbit's perigee is still within the atmosphere to ensure a predictable reentry of the core stage. As a consequence, the payload (or payload container) needs to carry its own means of circularizing the orbit. To provide propulsion and attitude control, Polyus used a modified FGB as its service module; with the maneuvering engines pointing "upwards" during launch, Polyus was programmed to perform a 180 degree flip and a 90 degree roll after separation before orbital insertion. Due to a programming error, the spacecraft initiated the flip but the thrusters did not fire to stop it. When the orbital insertion burn was initiated, Polyus was not oriented correctly and failed to reach orbit.
The second and so far last launch of an Energiya took place on November 15, 1988, after a launch abort on October 29, 1988. This was the first time that the "Buran" spacecraft, albeit unmanned, was to be tested in Earth orbit. The mission was successful.
Due to the subsequent collapse of the USSR and a lack of financial resources and payloads, the program was canceled. After being mothballed and stacked onto an Energia test article to be displayed in the Rocket Assembly and Test Facility (MIK RN) in Baikonur, "Buran" was destroyed in May 2002 when the roof of MIK RN collapsed, killing eight workers. "Buran" orbiters are outwardly similar to the American shuttle. However, there is one important difference. "Buran" has no main engines that must be used to launch the system into orbit. The two 11D58S engines on the orbiter are used exclusively for orbital maneuvers; a maximum of 14.5 t of kerosene (Syntin) and LOX can be stored in the Unified Propulsion System tanks.
