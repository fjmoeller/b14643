Cyclone-4M
 
 

KB Yuzhnoye * PA Yuzhmash
 

 

 

The new designed Ukrainian space launch vehicle Cyclone-4M is based in Canada

The collapse of the Cyclone-4 joint venture with Brazilia prompted KB Yuzhnoe to seek new partnerships that would give Cyclone-4 a new launch pad.
In 2016, the Ukrainian space agency gave formal permission to KB Yuzhnoe to proceed with the development of a joint venture for a new designed Cyclone-4M rocket in North America.
In February 2017, three US firms formed a joint venture called Maritime Launch Services, MLS, which was based in Halifax, the capital of Nova Scotia. MLS announced that it would build a launch facility in Nova Scotia, for the Cyclone-4M rocket.
The construction at the spaceport was promised to commence as early as 2018, if the MLS fund-raising effort goes as planned. According to the company, commercial launches from the spaceport could begin in 2019 or 2020.

The Ukraine-based KB Yuzhnoe design bureau began work on the preliminary design of a new launch vehicle.

The architecture of the proposed two-stage Tsiklon-4M (Cyclone-4M) rocket is built around the first stage booster derived from the US Antares and the Ukrainian Zenit rockets and burning liquid oxygen and kerosene. Both stages for Cyclone-4M were developed and produced in the Ukrainian city of Dnepropetrovsk, the home of KB Yuzhnoe.

The architecture of the former Cyclone-4 was drastically re-designed. The original booster stage of the rocket with a diameter of three meters, which was inherited from the Soviet Cyclone-2 and -3 launchers and the R-36 ballistic missile, was abandoned in favor of Zenit's and Antares' 3.9 meter caliber still in production and using non-toxic propellant.

While retaining the diameter of 3.9 meters inherited from Zenit and Antares, the new first-stage booster of the 39-meter-tall Cyclone-4M will be shorter to optimize it for its new role. According to original plans, the first stage of the new rocket was to be propelled by four modified RD-120 engines borrowed from Zenit's second stage, replacing the Russian-built RD-171 propulsion system on the first stage of the Zenit.

Although RD-120 was also developed at NPO Energomash in Moscow, it is serially produced in Ukraine and available for the new rocket. However due to a number of Russian parts, it is expected to be upgraded in Ukraine with indigenous components. As of March 2017, plans were made to equip the first stage of Cyclone-4M with a Ukrainian-built engine designated RD-870, which was previously proposed for the Antares rocket.

The second stage for Cyclone-4M will be borrowed from the third stage of the Cyclone-4 rocket. It will use a hypergolic (or storable) propellant. Although both of its components are toxic, they remove all time limitations of easily evaporating cryogenic propellants. As a result, the upper stage can be restarted multiple times and operate in space almost indefinitely, performing elaborate maneuvers.

The payload fairing for the new rocket will also be borrowed unchanged from Cyclone-4.

The chosen geographical location enables Cyclone-4M to deliver 3,350 kilograms of payload into a near-polar orbit, when launched southward over the Atlantic. Up to 5,000 kilograms could be delivered to a variety of low Earth's orbits with an altitude up to 600 kilometers.
