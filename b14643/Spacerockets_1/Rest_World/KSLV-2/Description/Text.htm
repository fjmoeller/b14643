KSLV-2 (Nuri)
 
 

Korea Aerospace Research Institute * Shinyoung Heavy Industries Co. Ltd. * Hanwha Aerospace * Koreanair Aerospace Division
 

The Korea Space Launch Vehicle (KSLV-2) is a 3-stage launch vehicle consisting of 1st stage with 4 clustered 75-ton liquid rocket engines, 2nd stage with a 75-ton liquid rocket engine and 3rd stage with a 7-ton liquid rocket engine under parallel development.
The Korea Space Launch Vehicle (KSLV-2) program consists of 3 continuous phases.
Phase 1 (Mar. 2010~Jul. 2015) activities include reviews of preliminary and system designs, construction of propulsion test facilities and ground test of 7-ton liquid rocket engine, which are successfully accomplished.
During the Phase 2 (Aug. 2015-Mar. 2018), currently underway, the detailed design of engine and the launch vehicle, ground test of 75-ton liquid rocket engine and launch of Test Launch Vehicle (for the verification 75-ton and 7-ton LRE performance) will be completed. The test vehicle that has one 75-ton and one 7-ton engines is scheduled to be launched in December 2017.
The final Phase 3 (Apr. 2018-Mar. 2021) aims the completion of the 3-stage launch vehicle system development effort, which will be followed by two trial launches to confirm the successful program accomplishment.
The rocket engine is the core element of launch vehicle technology. The development of a 75- ton-class liquid rocket engine and a 7-ton-class liquid rocket engine is the key agenda of the Korea Space Launch Vehicle (KSLV-2) program.
The 75-ton-class and 7-ton-class liquid engines to be mounted on Korea Space Launch Vehicle (KSLV-2) will be developed to the gas generator-turbo-pump type which will use kerosene as fuel and oxygen as oxidizer. For the development of rocket engine for the Korea Space Launch Vehicle (KSLV-2), 10 different propulsion (engine) test facilities (including Combustion Chamber Test Facility, Turbo-pump Test Facility, Rocket Engine Test Facility, Altitude Engnie Faciltiy, Propulsion System Test Facility in Naro Space Center) have been constructed in various locations. These facilities enabled the large-scale/high-thrust engine tests which were previously impossible in Korea.
These test facilities enable step-by-step tests of component performance tests for combustion chamber, turbo-pump and gas generators, which are key components of the 75-ton and 7-ton engines, and performance tests of integrated engines in ground and high altitude conditions.
The plan is to conduct tests (each requires around one week of preparation/test/analysis) on each component and engine anywhere between several dozens and 200 times.

2022, June 21 -- The second launch of the KSLV-2 (Nuri) with the satellite PVSAT (180 kg) and other payloads was successful. Thew planned SSO orbit has been achieved.

2021, October 21 -- The KSLV-2 (Nuri) with a dummy satellite on board has completed an unsuccessful maiden flight. The required final speed was not achieved because the third stage did not have the planned power. After a reduced burning time of about 475 sec, the final speed was 6.5 km/sec.

2018, Dec. 06 -- A new engine is being developed to enhance performance. It is a gas generator (!) engine used in the upper stage of future Korean launch vehicle.
A video is showing the test of the 9 t thrust engine. Burn time was 100 seconds, which was success.

2018, Nov. 28 -- The TLV was successfully tested in flight. After 151 sec of burn-time, a altitude of 209 km was reached.

2018, Febr. 06 -- The South Korean government postponed the launch of the KSLV-2 from December 2019 to February 2021. Still, it is going to launch a test vehicle (TLV). The test vehicle will be launched in October this year to this end and re-launched in October next year if the project fails.

2016, July 01 -- Korea has decided to postpone the test-launch of its next-generation space rocket, scheduled for late next year, due to some technical glitches.
The government had planned to complete the development of a two-stage test rocket by 2017 as part of a long-term project to produce an indigenous three-stage KSLV-2 rocket.
The Korea Aerospace Research Institute (KARI) said it will have to delay the test-launch for about 10 months. It cited a problem in the combustion of a 75-ton engine and more trouble in the welding of a fuel tank.
