MDA Interceptor
 

 

Missile Defense Agency

The Ground-Based Interceptor (GBI) missile is the weapon component of the Ground-Based Midcourse Defense (GMD) system, and consists of a multi-stage rocket booster (BV - Boost Vehicle) and a kinetic kill vehicle (EKV - Exoatmospheric Kill Vehicle) for exoatmospheric interception of ballistic missile warheads. The GMD system, originally known as the National Missile Defense (NMD) program, is one segment of the United States' forthcoming Ballistic Missile Defense System. As such it is managed by the Missile Defense Agency (MDA), formerly known as Ballistic Missile Defense Organization (BMDO).

Boost Vehicle-Plus (BV+)
The initial concept for the Boost Vehicle came from Boeing, and was called the COTS (Commercial Off-the-Shelf) booster, because it used developed and commercially available rocket stages. It was a three-stage design with an ATK GEM-40VN first stage and two Pratt & Whitney (UTC) Orbus-1A upper stages. However, the development didn't proceed as smoothly as expected, and the first test flight occurred on 31 August 2001. During this test a first stage anomaly occured, which could have prevented success in an actual intercept attempt. On the second BV flight test on 13 December 2001, the vehicle veered off course and had to be destroyed. Further flight testing of Boeing's BV was cancelled afterwards.
In March 2002, the GBI booster development program was restructured. Boeing's COTS vehicle was transferred to Lockheed Martin Space Systems Company, which developed an improved version known as Boost Vehicle-Plus (BV+).
The BV+ features a three-stage design with the second and third stages powered by ATK's Orbus 1A solid rocket motor replacing the motors originally supplied by Pratt & Whitney which exited the program before 2004. BV+ missile utilizes ATK's GEM-40VN rocket motor as the first stage.
In January 2004 the Boost Vehicle-Plus conducted successfully its first validation flight. The propulsion system based on Orbus motors was expected to be cleared before the end of 2005. The primary emplacement for BV+ missiles would be Alaska and California. Like the PLV, BV+ carries an exo-atmospheric kill vehicle warhead close to the threat. Then the kill vehicle heads to the target using its own propulsion system. Finally, executes the kill maneuver employing the vehicle's kinetic energy through force of impact. The BV+ should attain operational capability by 2006.

Orbital Boost Vehicle (OBV)
ATK Launch Systems supplies propulsion systems for all three stages of Ground-based Midcourse Defense (GMD) system boost vehicle under development by Orbital Sciences Corporation. Orbital Sciences Corp. (OSC) was awarded a contract to build an alternative booster (called OBV - Orbital Booster Vehicle) for the GBI. The Orbital Boost Vehicle (OBV) is based on the stages of a wingless Pegasus. It is an alternative launch vehicle for the GBI missile defence system. The vehicle uses slightly modified Orion motors manufactured by ATK Launch Systems and flown on the Orbital Taurus, Minotaur and Pegasus launch vehicles.

Kinetic Energy Interceptor (KEI)
The Kinetic Energy Interceptor is a U.S. Missile Defense Agency initiative to develop an interceptor missile capable of destroying medium-range, intermediate-range, and intercontinental ballistic missiles during the boost and ascent phases of flight. In late 2003, Orbital was selected as part of a Northrop Grumman-led team to conduct developmental and early-production phase design, engineering, manufacturing and testing for the Kinetic Energy Interceptors (KEI) missile defense program.
Raytheon is developing the kill vehicle, Orbital is designing the booster, ATK will provide the engine, and Aerojet will provide the Divert and Attitude Control System.
ATK Launch Systems supplies booster motors for all three stages of the Kinetic Energy Interceptor. The KEI Interceptor is 36 feet long and 36 inches in diameter. The first-stage and second-stage motors have been successfully testing as part of a series of static motor firings leading up to the first flight in 2008.
The KEI system will complement other U.S. missile defense systems that target enemy warheads in the midcourse phase of flight, including the Standard Missile 3 (SM-3) and the Ground-Based Midcourse Defense missile (GMD), both of which use ATK propulsion systems.

MSLS-PLV= Payload Launch Vehicle
 The PLV is developed for MSLS by Lockheed Martin and is a transformed Minuteman-II missile. This Exoatmospheric Re-Entry Interceptor Subsystem (ERIS) vehicle itself consisted of surplus Minuteman ICBM second and third stages.
The Payload Launch Vehicle (PLV), or GMD interceptor, is a booster used to fly the EKV designed to destroy ballistic missiles in midcourse out of the atmosphere. Once launched, the PLV will be directed to the interception area to intercept the incoming ballistic missile. Then, the EKV separates from the booster and guides itself to intercept the reentry vehicle.

 

The motors of retired Minuteman-I and -II missiles have been used since the early 1970s to assemble cheap high-performance rockets for experimental and test purposes, like boosting targets or interceptor vehicles in the ongoing ballistic missile defense development program. Examples for such vehicles include the Lockheed ERIS and HOE, Space Vector Aries, Coleman Hera, OSC SR19-SR19, OSC Storm-2 and Coleman SRALT/LRALT rockets.
